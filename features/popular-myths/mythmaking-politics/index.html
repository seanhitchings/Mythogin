Mythmaking in Politics

An apolitical analysis of myth as social technology: how narratives coordinate groups, create moral theater, and convert attention into legitimacy.

Audio Download

Thesis

What “mythmaking” does, regardless of ideology.

Mythmaking is the process of turning complexity into a story that feels morally obvious. The story produces identity (“who we are”), threat (“what endangers us”), and duty (“what must be done”). It compresses an infinite world into a few emotionally loaded symbols that people can coordinate around.

  • Identity: belonging, status, purity, the in-group face.
  • Enemy: a simplified antagonist that holds the system’s anxieties.
  • Ritual: slogans, hashtags, chants, spectacles.
  • Permission: “we’re justified” becomes “we must.”

Core Mechanics

How the narrative machine runs.

Keep this section concrete: show the moving parts. If you have examples, keep them neutral (or use historical examples). The goal is to make the pattern visible without turning the page into a partisan document.

  • Compression: a complex system is reduced to a single cause.
  • Moralization: disagreement becomes sin.
  • Overfitting: one story explains everything; contrary facts become “propaganda.”
  • Reward loops: outrage and certainty are rewarded with attention.

Antidotes

How to resist myth when it becomes coercive.

You can frame the “fix” as habits of mind: how to keep meaning without becoming possessed by narrative.

  • Multiple models: hold competing explanations simultaneously.
  • Steelman: describe the other side in a way they’d recognize.
  • Time horizon: ask “what happens in 10 years if we do this?”
  • Institutional humility: incentives distort perception; assume distortion.
