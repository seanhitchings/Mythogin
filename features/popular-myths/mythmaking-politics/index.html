Mythmaking in Politics

An apolitical analysis of myth as social technology: how narratives coordinate groups, create moral theater, and convert attention into legitimacy.

Audio Listen Download

Discussion

What “mythmaking” does, regardless of ideology: it turns complexity into a story that feels morally obvious.

Mythmaking is the process of compressing an infinite world into a few emotionally loaded symbols that people can coordinate around. The story produces identity (“who we are”), threat (“what endangers us”), and duty (“what must be done”). When it works, it organizes attention; when it overheats, it can convert disagreement into moral theater.

This feature aims to keep the analysis neutral: the focus is the mechanism, not the party. The same narrative machinery can be used to stabilize a society, to persuade it, or to possess it.

Identity: who we are

▾

Myths generate belonging: the in-group face, the shared values, the “real people.” Identity is emotionally efficient — it turns politics into kinship, and disagreement into betrayal.

Enemy: what threatens us

▾

A simplified antagonist absorbs a system’s anxieties. Complex failure becomes a single cause. Once an enemy-symbol is installed, the myth can run on autopilot: every fact becomes proof.

Ritual: how the story is rehearsed

▾

Slogans, chants, hashtags, spectacles: these are not “extra.” They are the myth’s reinforcement schedule. Ritual converts belief into a lived identity — and makes exit socially expensive.

  • Identity: belonging, status, purity, the in-group face.
  • Enemy: a simplified antagonist that holds collective anxieties.
  • Ritual: slogans, symbols, repetition, spectacle.
  • Permission: “we’re justified” becomes “we must.”

Impact

Why it matters: myths coordinate groups fast — but they can also distort perception and raise the cost of truth.

Compression makes action possible

▾

Political life is too complex to compute in real time. Myths provide heuristics that feel like clarity. This can be adaptive — but it also incentivizes the simplest story over the truest one.

Moral theater replaces problem-solving

▾

When disagreement becomes sin, policy becomes identity performance. “Winning” becomes more important than accuracy, and the public learns to signal virtue instead of reasoning.

Reward loops harden the story

▾

Outrage and certainty are rewarded with attention. Over time, the myth “overfits”: one story explains everything; contrary facts become propaganda; correction becomes betrayal.

  • Benefit: coordination, motivation, shared meaning.
  • Cost: distortion, polarization, truth becomes factional.
  • Failure mode: overfitting + moralization → coercion pressure.

Fix

Habits of mind that preserve meaning without becoming possessed by narrative.

1) Hold multiple models

▾

Force your mind to keep competing explanations alive. If one story explains everything, it’s usually too clean. Multiplicity prevents narrative possession.

2) Steelman before you criticize

▾

Describe the other side in a way they would recognize. This single constraint dissolves a lot of caricature-myths — and lowers the reward for moral theater.

3) Expand the time horizon

▾

Ask “what happens in 10 years if we do this?” Mythmaking tends to maximize short-term emotional payoff. Time makes tradeoffs visible.

4) Assume incentive distortion

▾

Institutions, platforms, and social groups reward certain perceptions. Expect distortion. This protects you from certainty-as-status games.
