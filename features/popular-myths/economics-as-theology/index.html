Economics as New Theology

How economic language borrowed the authority of religion — complete with sacred texts, moral judgments, invisible forces, and promises of salvation through growth.

Audio Listen Download
Transcript Read Download

Discussion

Markets don’t just allocate resources — they can become moral systems that tell us what deserves to live, what deserves to die, and what counts as “realism.”

Economics is a set of tools, models, and measurements. But in public life it often behaves like a theology: it offers a story about how the world works, what humans are, which incentives are “natural,” and what outcomes should be honored or feared. The language is secular — but the structure is devotional.

A priesthood of experts

▾

Economists and institutions can function like clergy: interpreters of complex texts, guardians of jargon, and arbiters of what counts as “responsible.” Expertise matters — but when it becomes unchallengeable, it stops being science and starts being authority.

Sacred texts and canonical models

▾

Certain models become canonical. They’re taught, repeated, and defended not only because they’re useful, but because they confer legitimacy. The map starts to substitute for the territory.

Invisible forces as providence

▾

“The market” is treated as a single will — rewarding virtue, punishing vice, and restoring balance. It’s a powerful metaphor, but it can smuggle in moral judgment as if it were neutral mechanics.

  • Salvation: growth, productivity, “competitiveness.”
  • Sin: waste, inefficiency, “distortions.”
  • Heresy: policies that violate the doctrine of the moment.

Impact

When economics becomes theology, policy becomes moral theater — and disagreement becomes blasphemy.

Tradeoffs become virtue tests

▾

Budget choices, wages, and welfare policies get framed as character: “deserving” versus “undeserving,” “disciplined” versus “irresponsible.” The debate shifts from outcomes to righteousness.

Legitimacy attaches to the story

▾

Institutions that speak the economic creed feel inevitable. Those that don’t are branded naïve. This can protect bad assumptions from scrutiny — especially in crises.

Sacrifice gets sanctified

▾

Pain can be framed as necessary purification: austerity as discipline, unemployment as “adjustment,” inequality as “incentive.” The story doesn’t just explain suffering — it justifies it.

Fix

The solution isn’t to ban moral language — it’s to separate moral aims from model worship.

1) Make values explicit

▾

Every policy has values baked in: whose risks matter, which harms count, what “success” means. Name the values up front so the debate isn’t smuggled in through technical terms.

2) Treat models as tools, not revelations

▾

Models are useful simplifications — but every simplification hides something. Encourage competing models, stress tests, and post-mortems.

3) Build accountability for predictions

▾

The fastest way to de-theologize economics is to demand calibration: what did we predict, what happened, and what did we change because of it?
