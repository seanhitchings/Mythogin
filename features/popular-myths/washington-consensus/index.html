The Washington Consensus

The global policy playbook that treated liberalization as common sense — and how its promises, tradeoffs, and backlash became a modern economic myth.

Audio Listen Download
Transcript Read Download

Discussion

A policy framework can become a myth when it’s treated as inevitable — not as a set of choices with winners and losers.

“The Washington Consensus” is shorthand for a cluster of reforms promoted in the late 20th century: fiscal discipline, market liberalization, privatization, deregulation, and trade openness. In practice, it often arrived as a single narrative: markets are neutral, the state is distortion, and growth will trickle into stability.

A universal script

▾

The mythic move is “one size fits all.” Differences in institutions, history, and capacity are treated as footnotes. The policy becomes the identity: modern = liberalize.

Technocracy as legitimacy

▾

Reforms are framed as “economic common sense” rather than political choices. That framing reduces democratic contestation — and increases backlash when costs land unevenly.

Promises and blind spots

▾

Liberalization can increase efficiency and capital flows — but it can also amplify inequality, hollow out industries, and weaken social trust if institutions aren’t ready.

  • Myth: markets are apolitical.
  • Reality: markets encode power and rules.
  • Tradeoff: speed of reform vs. social stability.

Impact

The Consensus shaped development policy — and the backlash shaped politics, from populism to protectionism.

Uneven gains

▾

When reforms deliver growth but concentrate benefits, legitimacy collapses. People don’t reject “markets” — they reject a story that denies their lived costs.

Sovereignty anxiety

▾

External conditionality can feel like governance by outsiders. Even good policy becomes politically toxic when it reads as imposed.

Boom-bust vulnerability

▾

Rapid capital openness without resilient institutions can magnify crises. The myth of smooth adjustment breaks when shocks hit.

Fix

Replace universal scripts with institutional realism: policy that matches capacity, history, and social contracts.

1) Start with institutions, not ideology

▾

Markets need rules, enforcement, and legitimacy. Build state capacity and accountability alongside liberalization — not after.

2) Sequence reforms

▾

Speed is not virtue. Gradualism can protect social stability and allow learning. The order of reforms often matters more than the slogans.

3) Pair openness with safety nets

▾

If the public pays the adjustment costs, the public will reject the project. Social insurance turns “reform” from punishment into transition.
